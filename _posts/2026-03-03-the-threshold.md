Liao has a theory about the threshold.

Modern chips — anything built after roughly 1995 — use transistors measured in nanometres. Hundreds of millions of gates on a sliver of silicon. The pulse killed all of them. Every phone, every server, every car built this century. Gone.

But the older stuff. The big stuff. Transistors you can almost see with the naked eye, etched on chips the size of your thumbnail. Micrometre-scale fabrication. Those survived.

We found three things still working:

1. A calculator from the supply office. Texas Instruments, probably 1982. It turns on. It adds numbers.
2. The CRT monitor in the old briefing room. The one everyone complained about. It glows when you give it power.
3. And this is the one that matters — the drone CPUs.

The military drone programme ran on custom processors. 16-bit, word-addressed, built to a spec from the 1980s. The brass called them "hardened" but really they were just old. Big fat transistors on big fat dies, because the contractor — Axiom Systems — had been building the same chip since 1984 and nobody ever asked them to shrink it.

Those chips survived.

The drones themselves are scattered across three continents. Their software is gone — the flash storage that held their firmware was modern enough to fry. But the processors are intact. Sitting in their sockets, waiting for instructions that no longer exist.

Liao says if we can find a way to write new programs and get them onto those chips, we'd have functional machines again.

I asked her what we'd write the programs with.

She said she'd get back to me on that.
