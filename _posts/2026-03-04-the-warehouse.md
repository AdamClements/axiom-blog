Martinez found the warehouse.

An Axiom Systems parts depot, about 40 clicks south. Brick building, no electronics in the door locks — just a padlock. Inside: shelves and shelves of components. New-old-stock, still in anti-static bags.

Processors. Dozens of them. TR-16, same as the drones. Also keyboards. Screens — Axiom PI-40 units, 40-column CRT displays, the kind of thing you'd see in an air traffic control tower in 1987. Motor controllers from a company called Ironhorse Industries. Radio modules. Scanner boards. All built to the same oversized spec. All functional.

And in the back office, in a filing cabinet: paper.

Service manuals. Schematics. The TR-16 instruction set, printed on actual paper, in a ring binder. Every opcode, every addressing mode, every memory-mapped register. Axiom Systems part number AS-TR16-REF-84, revision 7.

Liao held it like it was sacred text.

She said this was everything we needed. A processor, a screen, a keyboard, and a book that tells you how to talk to them. She said people used to build entire systems with less than this. She said the first moon landing ran on less computing power than what we were holding.

I asked her if she knew how to program in assembly language.

She said no. But she had the manual. And that's how everyone learned it the first time.
