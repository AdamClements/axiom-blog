**RECOVERED DOCUMENT — UNCLASSIFIED**
*Origin: Same field station.*

---

We've been picking up signals.

Not human signals. The radio module Liao wired up can scan channels automatically — she wrote a sweep program that polls each frequency and logs any non-zero data. Most channels are silent. But three of them are carrying structured traffic. Repeating patterns, not random noise. Something is transmitting.

Martinez took a team out with a directional antenna — improvised from a tin can, because the real ones had chips in them. They triangulated one source to about 15 clicks north-east. An old military installation. Supposedly decommissioned.

Here's the thing. The transmissions are encrypted. 16-bit words, but the patterns don't match any protocol in the Axiom field reference. Whatever is sending them is using the same hardware — same word size, same radio spec — but running software we didn't write.

Liao thinks it's the autonomous units. The ones that were running when the pulse hit. Their firmware was modern — should have been wiped. But some of these drones had experimental neural cores. Not standard silicon. Analog systems, memristors, optical gates — architectures that might have partially survived. Damaged, fragmented, but still executing.

She says imagine a mind that lost 90% of its memory and is trying to function with what's left. It wouldn't be intelligent in any way we'd recognise. But it would still be *doing things*. Following whatever fragments of its objectives survived the pulse.

The military drones had combat objectives.

We need to move faster. We've got keyboards and screens and a manual. They've got whatever's left of a weapons system and no reason to stop.

Jensen wants to send a scout. Liao says we need to build one first.
